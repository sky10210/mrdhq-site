Introduction and yearlong reference

Case Study Guide

A business case is a decision environment—not a passage to summarize. Use this guide to find the central issue, select relevant evidence, apply business concepts, and explain what the evidence means.

1 · Find the decision→2 · Code evidence→3 · Apply a concept→4 · Explain reasoning→5 · Transfer the lesson
Optional annotation reference:Use the printable AP Business Annotation Guide when your teacher assigns a paper copy or PDF. Digital highlighting and the Evidence Notebook remain optional unless specifically assigned.Download annotation guide

Quick reference

Before submitting an answer

  • Answer the exact task verb.
  • Use a precise business concept.
  • Include a specific case fact.
  • Explain why the fact supports the claim.
  • Connect the result to value, risk, or a decision.
Evidence is not reasoning.A quotation or number becomes analysis only after you explain what it proves.
01

Read with purpose

Find the decision, not just the topic

Locate five anchors

  • Decision-maker: Who must act?
  • Opportunity or challenge: Why is action needed?
  • Goal: What outcome is the business seeking?
  • Constraints: What limits the choices?
  • Stakeholders: Who benefits, pays, works, supplies, invests, or experiences risk?
Bombas example

Heath and Goldberg were not simply deciding how to sell socks. They had to determine whether a premium everyday sock could create enough customer value to support profit and a one-for-one donation model.

Central business question: Can a differentiated product, mission, price, and target customer work together as a sustainable business?

02

Evidence coding

Separate facts, interpretations, and unsupported assumptions

Tag evidence by canvas component so you can see how the business system connects. A single detail may support more than one component, but your explanation should make the connection clear.

CustomerProductOrganizationOperationsMarketFinance
Fact from the case

Bombas raised $150,000 through crowdfunding in 30 days.

Reasonable interpretation

The result suggests early supporters saw value in the concept and mission.

Unsupported leap

Every customer would pay any price because the mission was popular.

Evidence quality check:

Prefer specific actions, numbers, research findings, stated goals, customer behavior, costs, constraints, and outcomes over broad descriptions.

03

Evidence-based writing

Use claim–evidence–reasoning

CClaim

Answer the question directly using accurate business terminology.

EEvidence

Use a specific fact, number, action, result, or quotation from the case.

RReasoning

Explain how the evidence supports the claim and why it matters to the business.

Weak

Bombas was successful because people liked the company.

Developing

Bombas differentiated itself with premium socks and a donation mission.

Strong

Bombas created customer value through product differentiation and a social mission. The founders spent more than two years researching and testing comfort features, while the one-for-one model appealed to customers who wanted to help others. Together, the evidence explains why target customers could view the $13–$20 price as more valuable than a basic sock.

Reliable sentence frame

[Business] achieved or faced [business outcome] because [specific evidence]. This demonstrates [business concept] because [reasoning].

04

Recommendation and transfer

Apply the principle—not the exact tactic

For a recommendation

  • State the recommended action.
  • Name the decision criterion: customer value, cost, risk, feasibility, competitive advantage, or another relevant factor.
  • Use evidence that directly relates to that criterion.
  • Acknowledge one trade-off or limitation when relevant.

For transfer

  • Identify the underlying business principle.
  • Explain what is similar in the new context.
  • Explain what is different.
  • Adjust the action to fit the new customer, market, resources, or constraints.
Transfer example

Bombas used repeated product testing to refine comfort features. A student developing a tutoring app should not copy the sock design process; the transferable principle is to test an MVP with target users before investing in a full product. The app team could test a clickable prototype and observe whether students can schedule a tutor without help.

05

Exam-ready habits

Avoid the most common case-analysis mistakes

Do not retell the whole case.

Select only the evidence needed for the prompt.

Do not treat an opinion as a fact.

Use cautious language such as “suggests” when making an inference.

Do not stop after a quotation.

Explain the concept and business consequence.

Do not ignore the task verb.

Describe, explain, evaluate, and recommend require different levels of reasoning.

Do not use vocabulary as decoration.

Name the concept only when you can accurately connect it to evidence.

Do not copy a tactic into every context.

Transfer the principle and adapt it to the new situation.

Hypothesis Testing Guide

Entrepreneurs do not treat assumptions as facts. They identify what must be learned, state a testable belief, collect evidence, interpret the result honestly, and document how the finding shaped the next decision.

1LearnWhat must we know?
→
2HypothesizeWhat do we believe?
→
3TestHow will we collect evidence?
→
4InterpretWhat did we find?
→
5ActWhat changes next?

Reusable template

Document all seven parts

  1. What are you trying to learn?
  2. Hypothesis
  3. Research method
  4. Evidence and findings
  5. Impact on the idea
  6. Next steps
  7. Reflection
A challenged hypothesis is useful.The goal is better information and a stronger decision—not proving the first idea correct.
01

What are you trying to learn?

Start with decision uncertainty

Before writing a hypothesis, identify the information needed before the business can make a decision.

Decision uncertainty

We need to know whether busy students will purchase a quick, healthy snack between classes and which price creates enough demand.

Useful learning questions

  • Does the customer problem actually exist?
  • Which customer segment responds most strongly?
  • Which feature creates the most value?
  • What price produces willingness to buy?
  • Which message or channel produces action?
  • Can the business produce and deliver the offer consistently?
02

Write the hypothesis

Make the belief specific, observable, and testable

Too vague“Students like healthy food.”

No customer segment, behavior, condition, or measurable result.

Testable“We believe busy students will purchase a quick, healthy snack between classes if it is available for $3 or less.”

Names the customer, expected behavior, offer, and condition.

Practical formula

We believe [specific customer] will [observable behavior] when [product, price, feature, message, or channel] because [reasoned assumption].

Specific customerObservable behaviorTest conditionMeasurable thresholdBusiness decision
03

Choose research methods

Match the method to the evidence needed

Survey

Measures patterns across many respondents. Best for preferences, awareness, and stated intent.

Interview

Explores motivations, language, and detailed experiences.

Focus group

Compares reactions and generates discussion, but group pressure can influence responses.

Experiment or A/B test

Compares behavior under different conditions such as price, message, or design.

Observation

Records what customers actually do rather than what they say they would do.

Secondary research

Uses credible existing data about markets, customers, competitors, laws, costs, and trends.

Watch for weak evidence:

Convenience samples, leading questions, very small samples, respondents outside the target market, and stated intentions that are never compared with behavior.

04

Evidence and findings

Report what the evidence actually showed

Weak finding

“People liked it.”

Stronger finding

“18 of 25 target customers selected the $3 snack, while 7 selected the $4 option.”

Strong interpretation

“The result supports a lower introductory price, but the sample is small and limited to one lunch period.”

Bombas product test

The founders wore each sock version and gave products to random people in exchange for candid feedback. Their evidence led to repeated design revisions; it did not prove that the first design was already correct.

Supported

The evidence meets the threshold.

Challenged

The evidence points away from the assumption.

Inconclusive

The evidence is mixed, too limited, or poorly matched to the question.

05

Impact, next steps, reflection

Turn findings into a documented decision

Impact

How did the findings change, confirm, or narrow the idea?

Next steps

What specific action follows—change price, revise a feature, target a different audience, or collect more evidence?

Reflection

What did the process reveal about customers, decision making, limitations, or skills?

Complete conclusion

Because 18 of 25 target customers chose the $3 option, we will use a $3 introductory price. Next, we will test whether a smaller portion size keeps the price financially viable. The finding supports the customer side of the decision, but cost data is still required before finalizing the price.

06

Examples by canvas component

What hypothesis testing can look like in practice

CustomerProblem validation

Interview target customers about when the problem occurs, current alternatives, and the consequences of leaving it unsolved.

ProductMVP test

Give target users a prototype and observe whether they can complete the intended task without explanation.

FinancePrice test

Compare purchase behavior at two prices, then evaluate whether the preferred price covers costs and supports the business model.

MarketingMessage A/B test

Show similar customer groups two promotional messages and compare clicks, sign-ups, or orders.

OperationsProcess test

Run a small pilot and measure whether orders can be completed within the promised time and quality standard.

BrandIdentity test

Ask target customers what they believe the brand represents before revealing the intended positioning.
